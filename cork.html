cork

Ever since I went to New York on vacation with my family I’ve never wanted to be anywhere else. My dad brought me along on a work trip with him, just me and him because mom was working and my brother was still in school. We get along well. He’s kind of a hipster type, but in the most genuine way I can think of. That being said, he wasn’t going to take me to Times Square. Instead, he took me to Astoria, Queens, a tiny neighborhood on the east river that he had fallen in love with the last time he was there.


It wasn’t what I expected, to be honest. It was so much more beige than the image I had had in my head of what New York was “supposed” to look like. Every corner we stopped at smelled like a different kind of grilled meat, and the air was hot and full of voices shouting out in at least five different languages. We stopped in front of an equally beige building. The doorway was topped with a beat up awning, displaying its metal ribs from under the tattered tarp. The smell wafting out was intoxicating, like garlic and burnt cheese and more butter than I could even imagine.


When we walked in, the smell intensified. The tables were covered in chaotically patterned fabric. We sat down on well used plastic chairs, and my dad raised his hand and shouted out the names of foods I had never even heard of before, like he had done this a thousand times before. The food came out quick, on dollar tree plates painted with lightning mcqueen and disney princesses. Before I knew it, the table was full of steaming cheese breads, fat dumplings, and brightly colored salads. My dad leaned over to me as they brought a bottle of wine.


“You have to try this.”


It was sweet and fruity. I didn’t like wine, and usually just watched cluelessly as my dad would go on to his friends about how “this one is really dry” or “you can really taste the spice in this one”. But this tasted like nothing I had ever had before. The intricate designs on the cork caught my eye, and I picked it up. It was painted with delicate birds and twisting grape vines, intricate geometric patterns, and curved, unrecognizable lettering. I turned it around in my hand, the bottom was still wet with the dark red wine.


When we left the restaurant, the sun was beginning to set, turning the light brown of the landscape into a soft, dusty blue. The temperature had dropped a little, and I pulled my sweater tighter around me. I couldn’t stop looking around me, my face pointed towards the sky and the bright lights of the city as I walked. I felt pulled to the surreal dark blue of the dusk, and to the city itself. Something clicked in that moment, and I wasn’t sure if I’d ever be content anywhere else.
