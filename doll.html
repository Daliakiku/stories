doll

I think the most beautiful thing I own is a wooden doll my dad brought home for me when I was little. He wasn’t home very much, especially as I got older. I loved him, but I think it was very noticeable to him and to my brother and I that we were much closer to our mom. When he would come home, he would always smell like the airport (I hated that smell), chemical and clean in a way that is only actually clean on the surface. His breath always smelled like beer, but he never came home drunk or anything. He would open his suitcase and it would be half full of gifts and little candies, and my brother and I would eagerly wait as he would pass them out to us and mom.


I was very little when he gave me the wooden doll, but I remember he had given me a little explanation of who had made it and what it meant, as he always did. Then he continued, smiled, and told me he had seen it and thought it looked just like me. I smiled big, the doll was beautiful and hand painted with intricate designs. I was a chubby kid with uneven bangs and sticky fingers. I took it to my room and made it a little bed by lining its cardboard box with fabric scraps, and stuck a crown between the top of its little head and the walls of the box. I wanted to make sure it would be comfortable when I wasn’t playing with it.


A few years ago, I remembered for some reason that my dad used to have a blog. Maybe he had mentioned it casually in conversation over the phone. He laughed when I told him I had found it again. The majority of it was very unserious, as is his usual personal style. But beneath the dry humor and jokes about how all children are evil, there were stories from a young man, recently made father, who wrote in detail about every little thing his kids would do, his thoughts on the director of their preschool, on national holidays, on religion and culture and on ever-spreading internet culture, and on how he saw the world that his children were going to grow into.


He wrote about that wooden doll he had bought me when I was five. How he had bought it last minute before leaving Japan, and joked about compensating for his absence with presents. He wrote about the day a little five year old me had told him that I didn’t want presents, that I just wanted him to stay (I didn’t remember any of this of course). He instantly followed with another joke about how I was just figuring out ways to emotionally manipulate him out of his non-existent fortune. I read the whole thing in his voice, and I cried.
