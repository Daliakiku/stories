feather

The first person I ever fell in love with was my best friend. I loved how she always smelled like sweet spices, the way she always exhaled smoke through her nose, her colored tights, even the specific way she would breathe when she was really concentrating on something. I think she loved me too, at some point.


She was what I most regretted leaving behind. The day before I left, my friends had thrown me a small going-away party. I had tugged on her sleeve and led her out to the tiny gravel lined yard behind my house. There were already tears in my eyes as I handed a small wooden box I had painted and filled with drawings and little souvenirs from the 4 years of our friendship. She cried too as she opened it, and wrapped me in her arms tightly.


After she let me go, she leaned down and picked something off the floor. It was a tiny gray feather.


“I believe feathers have meaning.” She said shakily.


I nodded. I remembered she had told me that before.


“This one appeared here for you,” She handed it to me. “I think it means that we’re destined to see each other again. You’re really special to me, Dalia. I know that this friendship won’t end here. I’ll send you letters, okay? Every week.”


I nodded again, tears forming in my eyes. I knew she wouldn’t. But I hoped so much that she would. I kept the feather for years, it’s still in the same box I put it in that day when I ran up to my room looking for anything small enough so that I could carry it with me in the airport. It was a year before I could stop crying about her. I was so scared that she had never needed me the way I needed her. I think she would’ve written to me if she had.


She’s probably forgotten about the feather. There are some moments where I forget, too. At first I was obsessed with it. I was determined to make it into a necklace so I would never have to leave it behind, so I could carry her with me everywhere I went. I spent weeks doing experiments with resin and other “test feathers” I collected off the street. I would wash them carefully, and try again and again the perfect method to preserve the shape and softness forever. I can’t remember when I gave up, but I never dared to try with the actual feather she had given me.


I saw her again, 6 months ago. I’m not sure what inspired her to finally respond to my messages, and I’m not sure why I had kept sending her messages after so many years had passed. I knew it wasn’t going to be the same, and it wasn’t. We both knew whatever we had had died. I’m okay with that, but I still can’t bring myself to throw away that feather. Maybe one day I will, but I think it’ll be at least a few years before I start to live without a tiny, soft, gray pigeon feather on my bedside table.
